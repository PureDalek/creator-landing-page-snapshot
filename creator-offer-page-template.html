One Primary Job

The page should make one action obvious: subscribe, buy, book, download, start, or request help.

Enough Proof

Small creator offers can use lightweight proof: samples, screenshots, before-and-after copy, examples, credentials, or use cases.

Plain Fit Notes

Buyer-fit and not-a-fit copy reduces support friction and keeps expectations clear before checkout.

Copy-and-adapt page structure

Hero

Say who it is for, what it helps them do, and what happens after the click.

For [specific creator/customer] who want to [desired outcome],
[offer name] helps you [practical job] without [common friction].

[Primary CTA: Get the template / Book the audit / Join the newsletter]
[Secondary CTA: See sample / Use free scorecard]

Problem

Name the moment that makes the buyer want help now.

You already have [asset/page/offer], but visitors still ask:
- What exactly is this?
- Is it for me?
- What do I get?
- What should I click first?

What They Get

List concrete contents, deliverables, or outcomes. Avoid vague value words alone.

Inside:
- [Deliverable 1]
- [Deliverable 2]
- [Deliverable 3]
- [Example, template, checklist, call, report, or file format]
- [First step after purchase]

Proof

Use whatever proof is honest and specific. A preview can be proof.

Proof options:
- Screenshot of the product
- Short sample
- Before/after copy
- Creator credential
- Use-case example
- Public report or demo
- Tiny testimonial

Fit / Not Fit

This section prevents wrong buyers and gives right buyers confidence.

Good fit if:
- You have [starting point]
- You want [specific outcome]
- You can use [format]

Not a fit if:
- You need [excluded scope]
- You expect [unpromised result]
- You need [custom work not included]

Final CTA

Repeat the next action with a plain expectation.

Ready to [outcome]?

[Primary CTA]

After checkout, you will [receive/download/submit/book] [specific next step].
