How To Use It

Open your public offer page in one tab and this checklist in another. Mark every item that is already obvious to a new visitor. Anything you cannot mark is a candidate for your next edit.

First-Screen Clarity

The first screen names the exact audience, not just a broad market.
The headline says the buyer outcome in plain language.
The visitor can tell what is being sold without scrolling.
One proof signal appears near the first CTA: result, testimonial, customer count, recognizable client, or concrete sample.
The page explains who should not buy or who is not the best fit.

CTA And Buying Path

There is one primary CTA above the fold.
The CTA uses a clear verb, such as get, book, download, join, buy, or start.
A one-line expectation sits near the CTA: what happens after clicking and when.
Secondary links do not compete with the main action before the offer is understood.
The final section repeats the same primary CTA instead of introducing a new decision.

Offer Proof

The page shows what the buyer receives, not only what the product is called.
The strongest proof is close to the decision point.
Any sample, screenshot, preview, or deliverable is easy to inspect.
The offer avoids vague claims such as "grow faster" unless the page explains how.

Friction Scan

The page answers the buyer's biggest objection before the final CTA.
Price, scope, delivery, or next steps are visible before checkout.
Long paragraphs are broken into short sections that can be scanned.
The page removes one unnecessary choice, link, or repeated claim.

Quick Rewrite Prompts

Audience

This is for [specific creator type] who want [specific outcome] without [common friction].

Headline

Turn [current painful state] into [buyer-desired state] in [time frame or scope].

CTA Helper Text

Send [input]. Receive [deliverable] by [time frame].

Proof Placement

Move one proof point next to the first CTA before adding more content lower on the page.
